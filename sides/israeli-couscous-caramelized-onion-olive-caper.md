# Israeli Couscous with Caramelized Red Onion, Castelvetrano Olives & Capers

*A bold, briny Mediterranean side — chewy pearl couscous tangled with jammy caramelized onions, buttery olives, sharp capers, wilted greens, and a hit of lemon. Built on patience with the onions and technique with the couscous.*

**Serves:** 4
**Prep:** 10 min | **Cook:** 45 min | **Total:** 55 min

---

## Ingredients

- 1½ cups Israeli (pearl) couscous
- 2 medium red onions, halved and thinly sliced
- ½ cup Castelvetrano olives, pitted and roughly torn
- 2 tbsp capers, drained (reserve 1 tbsp brine)
- Zest and juice of 1 lemon
- 2 large handfuls of greens (spinach, baby kale, or Swiss chard), roughly chopped
- 1 cup grape tomatoes, halved
- ½ cup fresh flat-leaf parsley, roughly chopped
- 3 tbsp good olive oil, plus more for finishing
- Salt and freshly cracked black pepper

---

## Method

### Order of Operations
Start the onions first — they need 35–40 minutes and cannot be rushed. Toast and cook the couscous while the onions are in their final stage. Blister the tomatoes and wilt the greens in the same onion pan at the end.

### 1. Caramelize the Onions
Heat 2 tbsp olive oil in a wide, heavy pan over medium-low. Add the sliced onions with a generous pinch of salt. Cook low and slow, stirring every few minutes, for 35–40 minutes until deeply golden, jammy, and sweet — not just softened, but genuinely caramelized. Resist the urge to rush this with higher heat; you'll get bitter, unevenly cooked onions. Add a splash of water if they start catching before they've fully collapsed and sweetened.

### 2. Toast and Cook the Couscous
In a separate saucepan, heat 1 tbsp olive oil over medium. Add the dry couscous and stir constantly for 2–3 minutes until the pearls are golden and smell nutty — this step is not optional, it builds the backbone of the dish. Add 2 cups of well-salted water plus the reserved caper brine. Bring to a boil, reduce to a low simmer, cover, and cook 10–12 minutes until the liquid is absorbed and the couscous is tender with a slight chew. Fluff with a fork and set aside uncovered to steam off.

### 3. Blister the Tomatoes
When the onions are done, push them to the edges of the pan and crank the heat to medium-high. Add the grape tomatoes cut-side down and leave them alone for 2–3 minutes until blistered and starting to collapse — jammy and concentrated, not stewed. Fold the onions back in.

### 4. Wilt the Greens
Add the greens directly to the pan. Toss over medium heat for 1–2 minutes until just wilted and collapsed. Season with a pinch of salt.

### 5. Bring It Together
Add the couscous to the pan along with the olives, capers, lemon zest, and half the lemon juice. Toss everything over low heat until combined. Taste critically — you want briny, bright, and sweet in balance. Flat? More lemon juice. Lacks depth? More salt or a splash more caper brine. Finish with a generous drizzle of your best olive oil and all the fresh parsley.

---

## To Serve

Transfer to a warm bowl or plate family-style. A final crack of black pepper and an extra pinch of lemon zest over the top. Serve warm or at room temperature — this dish holds beautifully and arguably improves as it sits.

---

## Tips & Notes

- **The greens:** Spinach wilts fastest and disappears into the dish. Baby kale holds more texture and a little bitterness that plays well against the sweet onions. Swiss chard brings earthiness. Avoid full-grown kale unless you blanch it first — it won't wilt properly in the pan.
- **The olives matter:** Castelvetrano are essential here — buttery and mild, they play against the capers without competing. Kalamata will tip the whole dish salty and aggressively briny. Don't substitute.
- **Make ahead:** Holds well at room temperature for a couple of hours. If serving later, hold back the parsley and lemon juice and add just before serving to keep it fresh.
- **Pairs with:** Grilled fish, roast chicken, lamb chops. Also works as a standalone lunch with good bread and a fried egg on top.
